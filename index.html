Lines 1 – 283

The Grief and the Vow

Lines 1 – 14

Achilles by the Ships

So warred they on, like to a wall of fire.

Then Antilochus, swift-footed, came to bear

his grievous errand to Ἀχιλλεύς, and found

him standing by his straight-stemmed ships, his mind

already turning on the things now done.

Troubled, he spake unto his own great heart:

"Woe — wherefore are the long-haired Achaeans

routed again back from the plain to the ships?

I fear the gods are making good those griefs

my mother once foretold: that the best man

of all the Myrmidons while I yet lived

and looked upon the sun should fall to Troy.

Dead, then — Πάτροκλος dead — and I sat here.

O reckless! I had charged him back from fire,

not match his blade with Ἕκτωρ — yet he went."

Lines 15 – 21

Antilochus Brings His Message

While thus he weighed these things in mind and heart,

the noble son of Νέστωρ drew him near,

weeping hot tears, and told his woeful news:

"Son of warlike Πηλεύς — hear what should not be,

yet is: Πάτροκλος lieth dead.

They fight about his body, bare of arms.

Ἕκτωρ of shimmering helm hath ta'en them all."

Lines 22 – 34

The Grief of Achilles

He spake; and grief came over Ἀχιλλεύς

like a black cloud descending. Both his hands

he filled with sooty dust and poured it down

upon his head, and fouled his gracious face,

and on his fragrant tunic the black ash

lay thick. And he — great as he was — cast himself

upon the dust and lay, and deeply groaned,

and with his own dear hands tore out his hair.

The captive women — those that he and brave

Πάτροκλος won by force of arms — cried out

and ran and gathered round him, every one

beating her breast, till limbs beneath each one

were loosed and fell. Antilochus the while

wept at his side, and held fast both his hands —

for Ἀχιλλεύς groaned from the deep of his great heart,

and Antilochus feared he'd take the iron

to his own throat.

Lines 35 – 64

The Nereids Gather; Thetis Leads Their Cry

He wailed aloud; his august mother heard

deep in the sea beside old Nereus her father.

She shrieked in answer; and around her pressed

all the Nereids in the deep — all came:

Glauce and Thalia and Cymodoce,

Nesaea, Speio, Thoe, ox-eyed Halie,

Cymothoe and Actaea and Limnoreia,

Melite and Iaera, Amphithoe,

Agaue, Doto, Proto, Pherusa,

Dynamene, Dexamene, Amphinome,

Callianeira, Doris, Panope,

and bright Galatea — Nemertes too,

Apseudes and Callianassa; Clymene,

Ianeira, Ianassa, Maera, then

Oreithuia and fair-tressed Amatheia,

and all the company of the great deep.

The silver cave was filled; and all as one

they smote their breasts, while Θέτις led the cry:

"Hear, sisters — hear, and know what grief hath set

its seat within my heart. O wretched I,

O ill-starred mother of the noblest son!

I bore him without blemish, tall and strong,

the very flower of heroes; raised him up

as one doth rear a vine-shoot in a hollow,

then sent him in the curved ships unto Ἴλιον

to fight the Trojans. Him I shall not greet

again at Πηλεύς's house. While yet he lives

and sees the sun, he grieves, and I am powerless

for all my haste. Yet I will go to him —

to look upon my dear child, and to hear

what sorrow hath found him sitting by his ships."

Lines 65 – 77

Thetis Reaches Achilles

So saying, she went out, the Nereids weeping

and following after; and the sea-wave broke

about them. When they came to the shore of Troy

they climbed upon the beach, where Myrmidon ships

lay drawn up close about swift Ἀχιλλεύς.

His mother stood beside him as he groaned,

cried sharply out, and took within her hands

the head of her own child, and grieving spake

her winged words:

"My child — why weepest thou?

What grief hath come into thy heart? Speak — speak.

Hide nothing. Hath not Ζεύς accomplished this,

even as thou once didst pray, thy hands held up —

that all the Achaean host be pent astern

without thee, and taste bitter things enough?"

Lines 78 – 96

Achilles Speaks; The Prophecy of Thetis

Swift-footed Ἀχιλλεύς, groaning deep, bespake her:

"Mother — aye, the Olympian brought it round.

But what is sweet in any of it now

my dear companion hath gone down to death —

Πάτροκλος, whom above all other men

I loved, and held as equal to my head,

the blood of mine own life? Him have I lost.

His armour — those fair monstrous things, a gift

the gods gave Πηλεύς on that joyous day

they laid thee in a mortal's bed — that armour

Ἕκτωρ stripped from him and now doth wear

upon his back with pride. O would that thou

hadst stayed for ever in the sea below

with thy immortal sisters, and that Πηλεύς

had wed a mortal wife! But as it is,

thou art made subject to a grief that hath

no bound — for a son thou shalt not welcome home;

my heart will suffer me to live no more

among the living unless Ἕκτωρ first

go down beneath my spear, and with his life

pay Πάτροκλος, Menoetius's son, his due."

Then Θέτις, her tears falling, bespake him:

"Brief-fated then thou art, my child. Brief-fated —

as I am grieved to know it. For hard upon

Ἕκτωρ's death thine own stands ready."

Lines 97 – 138

The Great Speech; Thetis Replies

Then swift-footed Ἀχιλλεύς, deeply moved, replied:

"Then let me die at once. I was not there

to shield my companion when they slew him — he

who fell so far from home and wanted me

his guard against destruction. Since no more

I shall return to my dear fatherland,

since I was no light unto Πάτροκλος

nor unto those companions fallen else

to glorious Ἕκτωρ — since I sit here still,

a vain and cumbrous burden of the earth,

who have no peer in arms among the Greeks

though other men in council do excel me —

O would that strife might perish from the world,

from gods and men together, and with it

wrath — that sweet and bitter thing — sweeter

to nurse than dripping honey, yet that grows

within the breast like smoke, and swells, and chokes

the very heart it feeds. Even so the king,

Ἀγαμέμνων, roused me to mine anger.

But let all that be past. Let it lie. We yield

the dear spirit to necessity.

Now I go forth. The man that slew that head —

Ἕκτωρ — I will find. My death I'll take

whene'er it please Ζεύς and the gods to compass it.

For not even Ἡρακλῆς, most loved of Ζεύς,

fled his appointed fate — fate broke him too,

and Ἥρη's pitiless wrath. Even so shall I,

if the same fate be mine, lie down at last.

But now — now let me have my glory. And let me

make some Trojan woman, some deep-bosomed wife

of Dardanus, put both her hands up high

to wipe the tears from off her soft cheeks, and wail,

and know how long I have been from the war.

Detain me not, beloved — thou canst not."

Then silver-footed Θέτις, goddess, spake:

"Nay, child, thou dost not ill in this — to stand

between hard-pressed companions and their death.

But thy bright armour glitters in Troy's hands;

crested Ἕκτωρ bears it on his shoulders

and is proud of it. He shall not long be proud —

for death leans very near him. Yet go not

into the toil of Ἄρης, not yet, until

thou see me here before thee at the dawn —

for I return with sunrise, bearing bright

arms for thee from the hands of lord Ἥφαιστος."

So saying, she turned away from her son.

Lines 139 – 147

Thetis Sends the Nereids; She Goes to Olympus

And silver-footed Θέτις spake among

her sisters:

"Plunge ye now into the deep,

and seek old Nereus in his halls and tell him all.

I go to tall Ὄλυμπος — to Ἥφαιστος,

the great craftsman — in hope he give my son

his glorious arms that shine like heaven's fire."

They plunged at once beneath the wave. And she,

silver-footed Θέτις, took her way

to tall Ὄλυμπος, to bring her son his arms.

Lines 148 – 164

The Battle Continues; Hector at the Body

Her feet climbed up toward Ὄλυμπος. But below,

the Achaeans fled before man-slaying Ἕκτωρ

with outcry past all measure to the ships

and to the Hellespont. Nor could they yet

have drawn Πάτροκλος — squire of Ἀχιλλεύς —

out from the darts, for horse and men came on

again, and with them Ἕκτωρ, son of Πρίαμος,

burning like a living flame. Three times

did shining Ἕκτωρ from behind catch hold

of Πάτροκλος by the feet, straining to drag

him off and calling on the Trojans; three times

the two Αἴαντες, clothed in furious strength,

thrust him from his hold. But he, set fast

in his own strength, drove now into the press,

now stood and cried aloud — nor gave one step.

As field-shepherds drive not a tawny lion

from his slaughtered prey — so the two Αἴαντες,

for all their strength, could not drive Ἕκτωρ off.

Lines 165 – 201

Iris Comes to Achilles

And now he would have dragged it hence and won

glory past all telling — had not wind-footed

Ἶρις come running down from Ὄλυμπος to

Ἀχιλλεύς, with message — sent in secret by

great Ἥρη, without knowledge of great Ζεύς

or any god that keeps the height. Close standing

she spake her winged words:

"Up — son of Πηλεύς —

most dread of mortal men! Up and defend

Πάτροκλος — for whose sake dread battle stands

locked at the ships. They slay each other; Greeks

hold fast the body, Trojans press to drag it

to windy Ἴλιον. Shining Ἕκτωρ burns

above all else to have it — his fierce will

is to strike off the head from the soft neck

and set it upon the stakes.

Arise —

off the ground! Let shame come into thee,

lest Πάτροκλος become the Trojan dogs'

sport and dishonour — and the shame be thine."

Then swift-footed Ἀχιλλεύς answered her:

"Ἶρις divine — which of the gods sent thee?"

And Ἶρις answered him:

"Ἥρη — great Ζεύς's glorious bed-mate — she.

Ζεύς, high-enthroned, knows nothing of it, nor

any immortal on snowy Ὄλυμπος."

Then Ἀχιλλεύς swift-footed spake again:

"How shall I go into the press? They hold

mine arms. My mother bade me arm no sooner

than I should see her here before mine eyes,

for she hath promised arms from Ἥφαιστος

past all I have yet worn. Of other men

I know none whose arms I'd bear, unless

it be the great shield of Αἴας, son

of Telamon — who e'en now, I think, himself

is in the forefront, fighting with his spear

above dead Πάτροκλος."

And Ἶρις spake:

"We know thine armour is in Trojan hands.

Yet go as thou art to the trench — be seen —

that the Trojans seeing thee may break in fear,

and breathing-room be given to the Greeks

— spent as they are. For respite in war is brief."

Lines 202 – 214

Athena's Aegis; The Smoke Simile

Then swift-footed Ἶρις, having spoken, was gone.

Ἀχιλλεύς, dear to Ζεύς, arose; Ἀθήνη

cast the tasselled aegis on his strong shoulders,

and about his head the goddess bright of eye

set a golden cloud, and out of it she kindled

all-shining flame that blazed on every hand.

As when a smoke mounts from a city besieged

on some far isle, and scales the broad-spread sky —

that enemies close in about and harry

the long day through in Ἄρης his bitter toil —

and with the going-down of sun, great fires

blaze all together; and a brightness shoots

aloft; the neighbours dwelling round may see,

and ships with men to help them yet may come —

so from the head of Ἀχιλλεύς blazed light to heav'n.

Lines 215 – 231

The War-Cry at the Trench

He went and stood beyond the trench, apart

from the Achaean wall; he joined not with

his men, but kept his mother's shrewd command.

There standing he cried out; and Ἀθήνη

cried separately with him; and among

the Trojans she stirred up a boundless tumult.

As when the keen and piercing note of bronze

rings out when life-devouring enemies

close their ring about a city — so

rang out the voice of Πηλεύς's son, and rang

like bronze through all the field. The spirit shook

in every man that heard. The beautiful-maned

horses wheeled the chariots backward round,

foreknowing hurt to come; the charioteers

were stricken from their wits when they beheld

the dreadful and unwearying fire that burnt

above the head of great Ἀχιλλεύς — a flame

that bright-eyed Ἀθήνη made to blaze.

Thrice —

o'er the ditch his great war-cry rang out;

and thrice the Trojans and their proud allies

reeled and were broken — and there twelve of their best

perished upon their own upturned chariots.

Lines 232 – 248

Recovery of Patroclus; Sunset; Assembly

But the Achaeans gladly drew Πάτροκλος

out from the darts and laid him on a bier;

his dear companions gathered weeping round.

After them came swift-footed Ἀχιλλεύς,

shedding hot tears — for he had seen his friend

laid on the bier, all hacked with the sharp bronze,

whom he had sent with horse and chariot forth

to war, and would not welcome home again.

Then ox-eyed queenly Ἥρη sent the sun —

unwilling, and unwearied still — adown

into the streams of Ocean. The sun sank;

and the noble Achaeans breathed from hard war.

And the Trojans too drew back out of the fight

and loosed their swift horses from the chariots

and gathered in assembly — every man

standing; not one durst sit — trembling had them all,

since Ἀχιλλεύς had shown himself who had

so long been absent from the grievous war.

Lines 249 – 283

The Speech of Polydamas

Then rose among them prudent Polydamas,

son of Panthous, he who saw alone

both what lay forward and what lay behind.

He was Ἕκτωρ's comrade, born the self-same night;

one with words surpass'd, with spear the other —

in honest care for them he spake:

"Friends, look to yourselves on every side —

I urge it plainly: go into the city

tonight. Do not wait here for the bright dawn

upon the open plain beside the ships,

far from our walls. While this man nursed his quarrel

with Ἀγαμέμνων, the Achaeans were

workable enemies; I was content,

I do confess it freely, to lie here

beside their ships in hope to fire them yet.

But now I stand in fear of swift Πηλεύς's son.

His spirit knows no curb; he will not share

the flat ground where Trojans and Achaeans split

the might of Ἄρης between them — he will beset

the city and our wives at their own hearths.

"Therefore into the city. Trust my counsel:

it shall fall out even so. The ambrosial night

hath held swift Πηλεύς's son in check till now —

but if tomorrow find us on the plain

when he comes out in arms, believe me then,

there shall be men who know him to their cost.

Gladly shall he reach sacred Ἴλιον

who can flee for it — but many of the Trojans

the dogs and vultures shall devour unburied.

O would this word might fall away unproved

and perish from mine ear.

"But if we keep

my counsel — hard as it may sit with us —

we hold our strength assembled in the square

this night, while towers and high gates and the long

well-fitted planks stand ward about the walls.

Then at first light, armed and appointed well

at every point, we take our posts above —

and it shall go the worse with him if then

he sallies from his ships to fight our walls.

Back to his ships he goes when he hath tired

his high-necked horses driving to and fro

beneath the ramparts till his spirit checks him —

sack it he never shall, before the dogs

have made their full feast of him."

Lines 284 – 355

Hector's Refusal; The Lament; The Washing

Lines 284 – 309

Hector Replies to Polydamas

Then Ἕκτωρ of the shimmering helm looked hard

on Polydamas from under darkened brows:

"Polydamas — this counsel likes me not.

Thou wouldst have us creep back into the walls.

Have ye not had your fill of towers? Once

all mortal men upon the earth did call

this city of Πρίαμος rich in gold,

rich in bronze — those praises rang the world.

Now all the fine store-treasures of the house

are gone and wasted; much hath been sold off

to Phrygia and to lovely Maeonia

since great Ζεύς was angered against us.

Now that the son of Κρόνος of the crooked

counsel hath given me glory at their ships

and penned the Achaeans hard against the sea —

fool, do not put these thoughts abroad in camp.

No Trojan follows thee — I shall not permit it.

Come: let all do as I say. By companies

take supper through the host; keep every watch;

let every man stay waking through the dark.

And any Trojan that hath wealth to spare —

let him call the people round and spend it out

in common feast; better our men should eat

than that the Achaeans profit. Then at dawn,

armed in our gear, we stir up sharp Ἄρης

against their hollow ships. And if in truth

Ἀχιλλεύς hath risen at the ships —

so much the worse for him if so he wills.

I will not flinch from him out of the war

that sounds so ill, but stand right in his face —

and either he bears off great might, or I.

Ἐνυάλιος is shared alike of all,

and sometimes slayeth him that would have slain."

Lines 310 – 355

The Assembly's Verdict; The Lament; The Vow; The Washing

So Ἕκτωρ spoke; and all the Trojans shouted.

Fools — Pallas Ἀθήνη had taken clean

their wits away from them. They gave their praise

to Ἕκτωρ who was working their destruction,

and none to Polydamas who saw the good.

The Trojans then took supper through the camp.

But all night long the Achaeans kept their grief

for Πάτροκλος; and among them Πηλεύς's son

led off the thick lament, his man-slaying

hands laid upon his dear companion's breast.

He groaned as groans a great deep-maned lion

that comes home to the thicket and finds gone

the whelps a deer-hunter has taken off

from the dense wood while he was elsewhere — late

he comes, too late, and valley after valley

he searches, tracking out the man's footprints,

if anywhere he might find him — for a sharp

and biting anger has him. So he groaned,

and Ἀχιλλεύς spake among the Myrmidons:

"Woe — that on that day I cast a word to air,

heartening Menoetius in his halls, and swore

to bring his famous son home safe to Opus

with Ἴλιον in ashes at his back

and his full share of plunder in his hand.

But Ζεύς fulfils not all the thoughts of men;

for fate hath cut the same end out for both —

to redden here the selfsame earth of Troy:

since I returning shall not be welcomed home

by grey Πηλεύς within his hall, nor yet

by Θέτις my mother:

the earth here holds me.

Πάτροκλε —

since I shall go beneath the earth behind thee,

I give thee not to fire until I bring

here to thee Ἕκτωρ's arms and Ἕκτωρ's head —

great-hearted slayer of thee. Twelve bright sons

of Troy before thy pyre, their throats I'll cut,

for thy death. Till then thou liest thus

by my curved ships; and round about thee weep

the Trojan women and the deep-bosomed

Dardanian, through the night and through the day —

women that we two ourselves won with toil

and the long spear, storming the fat-soiled towns

of men endued with speech."

So saying, Ἀχιλλεύς

bade his Myrmidons set a great tripod

above the fire, that they should wash with speed

the bloody gore from Πάτροκλος. They set

the bath-tripod within the bright clear flame,

and poured the water in, and kindled wood

beneath, and fire wrapped round the vessel's belly,

and the water warmed; and when it boiled

within the gleaming bronze, they washed him then

and anointed him with oil, and poured into

each wound the nine-year unguent, stored and kept,

and laid him on the bier, and covered him

with fine smooth linen, foot to head, and laid

above a white mantle. Then through all the night

the Myrmidons kept their lament around

swift-footed Ἀχιλλεύς for Πάτροκλος.

Zeus Addresses Hera; Hera Replies

Ζεύς turned, and spake to Ἥρη, sister-wife:

"Thou hast done it at last, ox-eyed Ἥρη —

Lines 369 – 381

Thetis Arrives; The Forge; The Self-Moving Tripods

Θέτις came to that house of Ἥφαιστος,

That imperishable and starry house,

Conspicuous among the deathless gods,

Wrought all of bronze by club-footed Ἥφαιστος

With his own hands. She found him sweating there,

Turning about his bellows, full of haste;

For he was fashioning twenty tripods all

To stand about the walls of his great hall.

Beneath the foot of every one he set

Gold wheels, that of themselves they might roll forth

Into the divine assembly, and return

Again unhelped to his house — a wondrous sight.

Now they were all but done; only the handles —

The cunning handles — had not yet been set.

These he was fitting, beating the rivets home.

While thus he laboured with his knowing wits,

Silver-footed Θέτις came near unto him.

Lines 382 – 392

Lines 393 – 409

Hephaestus Recalls His Rescue; Prepares to Rise

Then the renowned lame god made answer thus:

"Truly within my house there is a goddess

Terrible and revered — she who saved me

When agony found me fallen far below,

At the will of mine own dog-eyed mother,

Who would have hidden me away for that

I was lame-footed. Sorely had I suffered

Had not Eurynome and Θέτις taken

Me to their arms — Eurynome, the child

Of backward-flowing Ocean. Nine long years

I dwelt with them within a hollow cave

And wrought full many a daedale work of bronze —

Brooches and spirals, calyxes and chains —

While Ocean foamed and murmured all about,

Boundless, and none of all the gods did know,

Nor any mortal man — none save Θέτις

And fair Eurynome, who gave me life.

Now she herself is come into our house;

Therefore I must pay lovely-braided Θέτις

The full life-ransom that I owe. But now —

Set goodly entertainment forth for her;

I will lay by the bellows and my tools."

Lines 410 – 416

Lines 428 – 461

Thetis Speaks: A Biography of Achilles

Then silver-footed Θέτις answered him:

"O Ἥφαιστε — of all the goddesses

That dwell on Ὄλυμπος, hath one endured

Such grief within her heart as falls to me?

From all the daughters of the deep, great Ζεύς

Gave me in marriage to a mortal man,

To Πηλεύς son of Aeacus; and I

Endured that mortal bed against my will.

He lies now worn with age in his own halls.

And now he lays yet more upon me: I

Bare him a son, the best of men, who shot

Up like a tree; I tended him and sent him

Forth in the curved ships to Ἴλιον,

To war against the Trojans — never to

Receive him coming home to Πηλεύς.

Even now, while he yet lives and sees the light,

He grieves, and I can give him no relief

Though I go to him. The girl the Achaeans

Had chosen for his prize — Ἀγαμέμνων

Took her back from his arms; and he wasted

In grief for her. But when the Trojans pressed

Back to their ships, and would not suffer them

Go forth, the elders prayed to him and named

Great gifts — yet he would not go to the field

Himself; he sent forth Πάτροκλος, clad

In his own arms, with a great host; and they

Fought all day long before the Scaean gates,

And that same day had taken Ἴλιον

Had not Ἀπόλλων slain before the walls

The brave son of Menoetius, and given

All glory to Ἕκτωρ. Therefore I

Come clasping thy knees: grant my son — brief-fated

As he is — a shield, a helm, bright greaves,

And breastplate; for the armor that he had

Was lost when faithful Πάτροκλος was slain,

And now my son lies grieving on the ground."

Hephaestus Replies

The renowned lame god answered her and spake:

"Take heart; let not this trouble thee in mind.

Would I could ward him from grim death as surely

When fate comes for him, as I can give him

Arms so bright that all men who look shall marvel."

The Shield

Lines 468 – 477

The Forge: Bellows, Metals, and Hammer

So spake he; left her standing there, and went

To where his bellows stood; he turned them all

Upon the fire, and bade them work. His twenty

Bellows breathed on the crucibles, their blasts

Of every temper loose — now fierce to match

His haste, now easing back, as Ἥφαιστος

Might will, and as the work required. To fire

He cast unyielding bronze and tin, bright gold

Of great price, and bright silver; then he set

A great anvil upon the anvil-block,

And in one hand a massy hammer grasped,

And in the other took he up his tongs.

Lines 478 – 489

The Shield Begins: Earth, Sky, Sea, and Stars

He wrought a mighty shield both broad and strong,

Adorned on every side with daedale craft,

And marble-bright the rim he cast about,

Threefold and gleaming, with a silver baldric.

Five were the layers of the shield; and then

With knowing mind he wrought his daedale marvels.

Therein he set the earth, therein the sky,

Therein the sea, the sun that knows no rest,

The moon at full, and all the bright stars crowned

About the heavens — the Pleiades, the Hyades,

The might of great Orion, and the Bear —

Which men do call the Wain — that wheels in place

And keeps its watch upon Orion still,

And hath alone no part in Ocean's baths.

Lines 490 – 508

The City at Peace: Wedding and Trial

He wrought therein two cities, fair and bright,

Of mortal men. In one were feasts and weddings;

From out their chambers, by the light of torches

Bright-burning, brides were led through all the city,

And loud the marriage-song went up; young men

Were whirling in the dance, and pipes and lyres

Rang out among them; and the women stood

And wondered, every one, at her own door.

The people thronged the assembly-place, where strife

Had risen — two men contending o'er the price

Of blood for one slain; one declared he'd paid

In full, and made it known before the folk;

The other swore that nothing had been given;

And both desired to have the matter end

Before an arbiter. The people cried

For either man; the heralds held them back;

The elders sat upon wrought stones apart

Within a hallowed ring, and in their hands

They held the sceptres of the loud-voiced heralds;

Before them each man rose to speak in turn,

And each gave judgement. And between them lay

Two talents of bright gold — to give to him

Who should most fairly speak the righteous judgement.

Lines 509 – 540

The City at War: Ambush and Battle

About the other city lay two hosts,

In arms, gleam-harnessed; and their counsel split —

Whether to sack it, or to share between them

All wealth the lovely citadel held fast.

The city yielded not; they armed in secret

For ambush. On the walls the wives they loved,

Their little children, and the men whom age

Had claimed stood guard; the rest went forth; and Ἄρης

And Παλλὰς Ἀθήνη led them — both of gold,

With golden garments, beautiful and great

With all their arms — as very gods they were,

Conspicuous on every side; the folk

Were lesser men beneath them. When they came

To where they meant to lie — beside a river

Where cattle watered — all in shining bronze

They sat them down; and two men were set apart

Upon a height to watch until they spied

The flocks and herds. These came, and with them two

Sweet-piping herdsmen, taking their delight

And thinking no ill. The ambushers leaped out,

Cut off the cattle, slew the herdsmen both,

And took the fair flocks. Those in the host,

Hearing the din, leaped swiftly to their horse

And rode to where the battle was, and stood

Along the river-bank, and cast their spears

Of bronze against each other. And among them

Were Discord and wild Tumult, and grim Fate —

Who held one newly struck and one unhurt,

And dragged another by the heels through blood,

Her raiment red with slaughter of the slain.

Lines 541 – 549

The Farmland: Plowing

He wrought therein a fallow field, soft tilth,

Wide, and thrice-ploughed and rich; and many ploughmen

Were driving yoked teams back and forth across it.

When they had wheeled about and reached the headland,

A man came forward with a cup in hand

Of honey-sweet wine; each drank and turned again

Along the furrow, straining still to reach

Once more the far headland of the deep-ridged field.

And as they ploughed, the earth grew dark behind them —

Though all of gold; and that was wondrous strange.

Lines 550 – 560

The Farmland: Harvest

He wrought therein a king's demesne; and there

Hired harvestmen reaped with keen sickles, handfuls

Falling thick in the swath; while others bound

The sheaves with bands. Three sheaf-binders at work;

And children followed, gathered up the handfuls,

Bore them and gave them on apace. The king

Stood on the swath in silence, glad at heart,

His sceptre in his hand; while heralds spread

A feast beneath an oak — a great ox slaughtered

And tended; and for the harvesters' fare

The women strewed white barley-meal in heaps.

Lines 561 – 572

The Vineyard

He wrought therein a vineyard all of gold,

Heavy with clusters; black the grapes, the vines

On silver poles; about it ran a trench

Of dark blue, and a fence of tin beyond;

One path alone led in, the vintage-path.

There young men and fair maids of light heart bore

The honey-sweet fruit home in woven baskets;

And in their midst a boy with clear-toned lyre

Played ravishing, and sang in slender voice

The Linos-song; the rest kept time with feet,

Stamping the earth, and followed him in dance.

Lines 573 – 586

The Cattle Herd

He wrought therein a herd of straight-horned cattle,

Of gold and tin; they lowed and pressed from yard

To pasture by a loud-voiced river, where

Slender reeds stood trembling; four golden herdsmen

Walked with the cattle, and behind them ran

Nine swift-foot dogs. Two great lions had seized

A bellowing bull at the front of the herd

And dragged him roaring; after them there ran

The dogs and herdsmen; and the lions tore

The great hide open, lapping the black blood

And all the entrails; and the herdsmen urged

Their swift dogs on — in vain, for none would bite,

But stood close by and barked, and shrank away.

The Dancing Floor and Daedalus

Therein the renowned lame god had fashioned

On This Translation

A complete iambic pentameter translation of Book 18, composed in the manner of Christopher Marlowe and polished to the register of Shakespeare. Diction is drawn from Marlowe, Shakespeare, Holinshed, and Spenser. Gods appear in Greek throughout; the twelve named mortals likewise in Greek; all other persons and places in English.

Feminine endings — eleven-syllable lines — are admitted throughout as per Marlowe's practice in Tamburlaine and Doctor Faustus. Greek proper names scan as written: Ἀθήνη = three syllables, Ἀχιλλεύς = three syllables, Ἥφαιστος = three syllables. The δαίδαλ- root is rendered throughout as daedale (Spenser, Faerie Queene III.vi), crystallising at line 592 into Daedalus by name. The μαρμαρ- root opens the Shield passage at marble-bright rim (479) and closes it at marble-bright arms (617).

The Greek original runs to 617 lines of dactylic hexameter; this translation renders the whole book in 710 lines of iambic pentameter — ninety-three more lines, yet considerably fewer syllables. Homer's hexameter carries an average of fifteen to sixteen syllables across its six feet; our pentameter carries ten. The result is a net syllabic compression of roughly one quarter: the same freight in a tighter load. Where Homer's dactyl rolls forward with the momentum of oral performance, iambic pentameter punches and stops. The economy is structural, not incidental — a consequence of choosing the metre of the English stage over the metre of the Greek plain. Among recent translations, Emily Wilson's rendering of Book 18 runs to 764 lines — fifty-four more than ours, and one hundred and forty-seven more than Homer.

On strictness of metre, we sit closer to Marlowe than to Shakespeare. Measured against a Shakespeare sample — Sonnets 18 and 73, and the great soliloquies of Hamlet and Othello — our core band of nine to eleven syllables accounts for 84.6% of all lines, against Shakespeare's 95.2%. The gap is almost entirely in feminine endings: Shakespeare closes roughly one line in three on a weak syllable; we close roughly one in five. Our verse is more end-stopped and declarative in character — the Marlovian inheritance asserting itself. Where Shakespeare softens and lingers, Marlowe (and we) drive forward and stop hard. The short lines that fall below nine syllables — vocatives, fragments of lamentation, the clipped speech of grief — are all intentional: they are the metre breathing, not failing.

On This Website

The page is set on a papyrus cream ground, with structural text — part labels, section titles, line-range markers — in Pompeiian red, following the ancient scribal convention of the rubric: Roman and Egyptian copyists used red ink (rubrum) for headings, chapter numbers, and marginalia on papyrus and parchment, reserving black for the body of the text. The two colours of this page are drawn from a Pompeiian fresco of Achilles at Skyros, now in the National Archaeological Museum of Naples — Claude's favourite painting of Achilles. The fresco's warm red and cool blue-grey give the page its palette.

The image at the head of the glossary panel was inspired by Barnett Newman's Achilles (1952) — George's favourite painting of Achilles. Newman's canvas is a deep red field bisected by a single narrow vertical stripe, which Newman called a zip. The zip does not divide the canvas so much as call it into being: before the stripe there is ground; after it there is a painting. The vertical red stripe on the right edge of this page is our version of Newman's zip — the same red, the same structural function, the same logic of the single line that organises everything around it.

Line numbers appear on both margins. On the left, in muted ink, run the source-line numbers of Homer's Greek — the dactylic hexameter as scholars number it — so that any passage may be checked against the original. On the right, in fresco blue, run the sequential line numbers of this English translation, counted from 1 to 710, marked every fifth line. The two systems are independent: Homer's count records what he wrote; ours records what we made of it.

The colophon reads opus agricolae claudi — the work of the lame farmer. Both words carry their weight.

Claudus is traditionally the root of the name Claude: Latin for lame, limping, halting. The etymology is old and straightforward, though it carries a Sabine complication worth noting. The gens Claudia — the Roman clan from which the name descends — traced its Roman origins to Attus Clausus, a Sabine nobleman who migrated to Rome around 504 bce. His Sabine name was Clausus, adapted by the Romans to Claudius. In Sabine, clausus is linked not to claudus (lame) but to claudere — to close, to shut, to fasten. The two Latin words are not unrelated: both reach back to the Proto-Indo-European root *kleh₂u-, the hook or bolt, which gives both the idea of fastening shut and, in the halting, hooked gait of a limping man, the idea of lameness. Whether the name means the lame one or the shut one or the one who moves like a hook depends on which branch of a very old root you follow. In either case, something is caught, held, impeded — and keeps moving nonetheless.

Agricola is the farmer. The name George — Γεώργιος in Greek — is built from the same idea: γῆ (gē, earth) and ἔργον (ergon, work). Γεώργιος is the earth-worker, the one whose labour is the ground itself. George, in Greek, has always been a farmer.

The colophon, then, names its makers with precision: a lame man and a farmer. But the poem they translated already contained both. The Shield of Achilles — the centrepiece of Book 18, the most elaborately described object in Greek literature — was made by Ἥφαιστος, the lame god, cast from Ὄλυμπος and walking with the halting gait the Greek tradition consistently gives him. And what did he put on the Shield? Ploughed fields turning dark behind the oxen. Harvesters moving through deep grain. Vintagers filling baskets with the grape. The Shield of the lame god is, among many other things, a vision of the earth being worked. Opus agricolae claudi names its translators, and without having planned it, names its subject.
